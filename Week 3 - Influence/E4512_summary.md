# Summary: The Search Engine Manipulation Effect (SEME) and Its Possible Impact on the Outcomes of Elections

**Authors:** Robert Epstein and Ronald E. Robertson  
**Affiliation:** American Institute for Behavioral Research and Technology  
**Published:** PNAS, August 4, 2015

## Overview
This paper investigates the *Search Engine Manipulation Effect (SEME)*, a phenomenon where biased search engine rankings shift the preferences of undecided voters. Through five randomized controlled experiments involving over 4,500 participants in the United States and India, the authors demonstrate that search algorithms can significantly influence election outcomes, often without voters' awareness.

## Key Findings

### 1. Magnitude of Influence (VMP)
The study establishes a metric called **Vote Manipulation Power (VMP)**, defined as the percentage increase in the number of undecided voters favoring a candidate after being exposed to biased search rankings.
*   **SEME is powerful:** Biased rankings shifted the voting preferences of undecided voters by **20% or more**.
*   **Demographic variability:** The effect was not uniform; in some demographic groups, the shift was dramatically higher (up to **80%**).
*   **Close elections:** Given that many elections are won by small margins (often <3%), SEME has the potential to determine the outcomes of a substantial number of elections worldwide.

### 2. Invisibility of Manipulation
*   **Masking Bias:** The authors found that bias could be effectively masked. Even simple masking (e.g., swapping a few search results) kept most users unaware of the manipulation.
*   **Awareness does not inoculate:** Surprisingly, even when subjects *were* aware of the biased rankings, the manipulation often still worked. In some cases, awareness appeared to **enhance** the effect, possibly because users trust search algorithms to present the "best" or "most popular" results, interpreting the ranking as a signal of quality or viability.

### 3. Study Breakdown

#### Study 1: Laboratory Experiments (San Diego, CA)
*   **Context:** Mock search engine ("Kadoodle") used to research candidates for the 2010 Australian election (Tony Abbott vs. Julia Gillard), ensuring US subjects were "undecided" due to unfamiliarity.
*   **Method:** Subjects were shown rankings biased toward one candidate, the other, or neither (control).
*   **Result:** Preferences shifted significantly toward the candidate favored by the search rankings.

#### Study 2: National Online Experiment (USA)
*   **Context:** Large-scale replication using Amazon Mechanical Turk (N=2,100).
*   **Method:** Used a diverse sample across all 50 US states.
*   **Result:** Replicated finding of Study 1. Identified specific demographic vulnerabilities (e.g., moderate Republicans were highly susceptible).

#### Study 3: Real-World Election (India)
*   **Context:** Conducted during the 2014 Lok Sabha elections in India (Gandhi vs. Kejriwal vs. Modi).
*   **Method:** Recruited undecided voters in India.
*   **Result:** SEME was effective even with real voters familiar with the candidates. VMP was **10.6%** overall, rising to **24.5%** in certain conditions (optimized rankings and excluding oppositional voters).

## Implications for Democracy

### Unregulated Power
*   Search engines, often operating as monopolies in specific regions, possess the capability to influence elections with impunity.
*   This influence is essentially a form of **"digital gerrymandering"** or a **"digital bandwagon effect,"** where algorithmic choices (intentional or organic) create a self-fulfilling prophecy of candidate popularity.

### Regulatory Challenges
*   **Detection is difficult:** Because search rankings can be customized at the individual level, it is nearly impossible for regulators to detect systematic bias. Rankings might appear neutral to a regulator while being heavily biased for specific vulnerable voters.
*   **The "Organic" Defense:** Even if bias is detected, companies can claim it is the result of "organic" algorithm dynamics driven by user behavior rather than deliberate manipulation.

## Conclusion
The authors conclude that unregulated search rankings pose a significant threat to the democratic system. The combination of proprietary algorithms, high user trust, and the ability to target specific undecided voters grants search engine companies an unprecedented power to shape electoral outcomes without public accountability.
