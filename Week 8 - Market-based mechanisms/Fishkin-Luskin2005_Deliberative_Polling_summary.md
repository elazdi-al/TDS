# Experimenting with a Democratic Ideal: Deliberative Polling and Public Opinion

## Summary of Fishkin & Luskin (2005)

**Authors:** James S. Fishkin (Stanford University) & Robert C. Luskin (University of Texas at Austin)  
**Published in:** Acta Politica, 2005, Vol. 40, pp. 284–298

---

## Core Thesis

The paper presents **Deliberative Polling** as a practical methodology that successfully combines two historically competing democratic values: **political equality** and **deliberation**. While democratic reforms have expanded political equality (through expanded suffrage, referenda, primaries), this has paradoxically diminished deliberation, as mass publics typically know and think little about politics. Deliberative Polling offers an innovative solution by creating conditions under which ordinary citizens can engage in meaningful, informed deliberation while maintaining political equality through random sampling.

---

## Key Concepts

### Political Equality
- Defined as **equal consideration of everyone's preferences** within a relevant population
- Achieved through processes where everyone has the same "voting power" (equal chance of being the decisive voter)
- Can be maintained even when voters are a subset of the population **selected by lot** (random sampling)

### Deliberation
Defined as **weighing of competing considerations through discussion** that is:
1. **Informed (and informative):** Arguments supported by accurate factual claims
2. **Balanced:** Arguments met by contrary arguments
3. **Conscientious:** Participants willing to talk and listen with civility and respect
4. **Substantive:** Arguments considered sincerely on their merits, not on who makes them
5. **Comprehensive:** All significant viewpoints receive attention

These criteria are **procedural**, not requiring any particular style of thought or acceptance of given premises.

---

## The Tension Between Political Equality and Deliberation

### The Problem with Mass Democracy
- As political equality has increased (expanded franchise, referenda, primaries), **deliberation has decreased**
- Most citizens know and think remarkably little about politics
- Decisions by referendum involve far less deliberation than those by legislatures
- People typically discuss politics with others who are very similar and share similar views (creating **echo chambers**)

### Limitations of Elite Deliberation
- Elite-level politics involves much **posturing and negotiation** rather than genuine deliberation
- Legislators are bound by constituencies and parties, constraining genuine deliberation
- Ironically, ordinary citizens may have **greater potential for real deliberation** because they are not bound by constituencies or parties

### The Failure of Conventional Polling
- George Gallup envisioned polling as a "sampling referendum" that would scale up New England town meeting democracy
- While polling achieves **political equality** through random sampling, it captures no real deliberation
- Typical survey responses are **"nonattitudes"** or **"minimal attitudes"**—cognitively threadbare, top-of-the-head responses
- Opinions reflect normal levels of inattention and disengagement, not informed reflection

## Deliberative Polling: The Methodology

### Design Structure
1. **Random Sample Selection:** Draw and interview a random sample from the population
2. **Briefing Materials:** Participants receive carefully balanced materials laying out major arguments for and against policy proposals, vetted by an advisory board for balance and accuracy
3. **Deliberative Weekend:** Participants brought to a single site (typically for a weekend) to deliberate
4. **Small Group Discussions:** Randomly assigned groups with trained moderators who:
   - Maintain civility and mutual respect
   - Encourage diffident participants
   - Restrain loquacious participants
   - Ensure all major arguments get aired
5. **Plenary Sessions:** Participants put questions to balanced panels of policy experts and policymakers
6. **Post-Deliberation Questionnaire:** Opinions measured before (initial interview) and after deliberation

### Two Phases of Deliberation
| Phase | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| **Anticipatory (at-home)** | Socially homogeneous; imbalanced; discussions with family, friends, coworkers; increased media attention |
| **On-site (weekend)** | Balanced (ensured by moderators); heterogeneous discussion partners (via random assignment); closer to the deliberative ideal |

### Historical Precedent
- Deliberative microcosms chosen by lot made key decisions in **ancient Athens**
- The practice fell into oblivion for millennia
- Modern polling revived random sampling but without deliberation
- Deliberative Polling **recombines both elements**

## Three Critiques of Deliberative Democracy—and Responses

### 1. The "Defeatist" Critique
**Claim:** Deliberation is **impossible**. The public is too confused, inconsistent, and ignorant to be worth consulting (Schumpeter, 1942; Posner, 2004).

**Evidence Against This Critique:**
- Opinion changes are **uninfluenced by sociodemographic factors** (including education), suggesting the process is accessible to all strata
- Participants gain substantial information (mean gains of ~10% on factual items are common)
- Opinion changes are **driven by information gains**—those who learn the most change the most
- Deliberation **increases single-peakedness** (reducing the probability of Arrovian preference cycles), countering claims that consultation leads to confusion

### 2. The "Extenuationist" Critique
**Claim:** Deliberation is **unnecessary**. People can use heuristics and shortcuts to approximate their informed preferences (Popkin, 1992; Lupia & McCubbins, 1998).

**Evidence Against This Critique:**
- **Opinions often change significantly** after deliberation (over half of policy attitude items show statistically significant net change)
- **Vote intentions change even more dramatically** (e.g., Liberal Democrats gained ~20% in a British Deliberative Poll; Australian Republic support increased 16%)
- Post-deliberation attitudes are **more predictable and hinge more on normatively desirable criteria**
- Changes are **information-driven**, proving that initial positions were not where people "really wanted to be"

### 3. The "Alarmist" Critique
**Claim:** Deliberation is **harmful**—it advantages certain groups' preferences (Sanders, 1997) and causes **group polarization** (Sunstein, 2003).

**Evidence Against This Critique:**
- Roughly **half of small groups polarize, half do not**—no systematic pattern supporting "law of group polarization"
- Preferences **neither consistently homogenize nor polarize** within groups
- Balanced deliberation promotes **balanced learning** (Danish euro referendum study showed participants learned facts favorable to both sides during balanced weekend deliberation, unlike the imbalanced pre-weekend period)
- Moderators enforce balance, preventing argument pools from reflecting initial opinion distributions
- Overwhelming majorities of participants report that discussions were **not dominated by a few people**
- Changes in opinion are **unrelated to social location** (no tendency for certain groups to disproportionately influence others)

---

## Key Empirical Findings

| Finding | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Representativeness** | Participants remain representative despite self-selection; differences from non-participants are few and modest |
| **Opinion Change** | Substantial individual-level and net change; over half of policy items show significant shifts |
| **Vote Intention Change** | Often larger and more significant than policy attitude changes |
| **Information Gains** | Impressive learning; rare items don't show significant gains; some gains are dramatic (e.g., awareness increased from 16% to 73% in Australian referendum) |
| **Information-Driven Change** | Those who learn the most disproportionately account for net opinion change |
| **No Social Location Bias** | Changes unrelated to demographics; no group dominates the direction or magnitude of change |
| **More Coherent Preferences** | Post-deliberation attitudes are more predictable from values and empirical premises |
| **Increased Single-Peakedness** | Reduces probability of Arrovian preference cycles; driven by most-informed participants |
| **No Systematic Polarization** | Groups do not consistently polarize or homogenize |
| **Balanced Learning under Balance** | Balanced deliberation leads participants to learn facts supporting both sides |

## Validity Considerations

### Internal Validity
- Concerns whether observed changes truly stem from the deliberative treatment
- Control groups (non-deliberating random samples) provide assurance against contemporaneous external influences
- Changes are tied to information gains, strengthening causal inference

### External Validity
- Concerns whether results generalize to the broader population
- Random sampling ensures the sample resembles the population
- If the whole public could be subjected to the same treatment, similar results would be expected

---

## Outstanding Questions

The authors acknowledge that many questions remain about the **individual elements of the deliberative treatment**:
- How much does each quality of deliberation (balance, information, etc.) matter?
- What difference would real-world levels of imbalance make?
- How much do moderators and group heterogeneity contribute?
- How much effect occurs during anticipatory vs. on-site deliberation?
- What role do briefing materials play?
- Would less confidential opinion harvesting affect polarization?

---

## Theoretical Significance

### Moving from Thought Experiments to Real Experiments
- Political theory has relied on **thought experiments** about what people would decide under ideal conditions (e.g., Rawls's "original position")
- Deliberative Polling allows for **real social science experiments** testing these counterfactuals
- The treatment intentionally creates a normatively relevant counterfactual—participants deliberate more intensively and with more balance than in real life
- Results have **recommending force** to policymakers and the public as evidence of what informed, thoughtful citizens would prefer

### Implications for Democratic Theory
- Confirms that **ordinary people can deliberate effectively**
- Demonstrates that **deliberation makes a meaningful difference** to preferences
- Shows that deliberation **need not polarize or privilege** certain groups
- Suggests that combining political equality with deliberation is **practically achievable**, not just theoretically desirable

---

## Conclusion

Deliberative Polling represents a successful empirical exploration of deliberative democracy that counters all three major critiques:
- **Against defeatism:** Ordinary citizens *can* deliberate effectively when given the opportunity
- **Against extenuationism:** Deliberation *does* change preferences significantly—people learn and update their views
- **Against alarmism:** Deliberation *need not* polarize or bias outcomes when properly structured with balance

The methodology demonstrates that the apparently conflicting values of political equality and deliberation can be combined in practice, offering both a research tool and a potential democratic reform that gives voice to what the public *would* think if given the chance to learn and discuss.
