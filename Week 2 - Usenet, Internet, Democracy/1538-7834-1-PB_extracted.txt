Abstract
Directed links in social media could represent anything
from intimate friendships to common interests, or even
a passion for breaking news or celebrity gossip. Such
directed links determine the Ô¨Çow of information and
hence indicate a user‚Äôs inÔ¨Çuence on others‚Äîa concept
that is crucial in sociology and viral marketing. In this
paper, using a large amount of data collected from Twitter, we present an in-depth comparison of three measures of inÔ¨Çuence: indegree, retweets, and mentions.
Based on these measures, we investigate the dynamics of user inÔ¨Çuence across topics and time. We make
several interesting observations. First, popular users
who have high indegree are not necessarily inÔ¨Çuential
in terms of spawning retweets or mentions. Second,
most inÔ¨Çuential users can hold signiÔ¨Åcant inÔ¨Çuence over
a variety of topics. Third, inÔ¨Çuence is not gained spontaneously or accidentally, but through concerted effort
such as limiting tweets to a single topic. We believe that
these Ô¨Åndings provide new insights for viral marketing
and suggest that topological measures such as indegree
alone reveals very little about the inÔ¨Çuence of a user.

Introduction
InÔ¨Çuence has long been studied in the Ô¨Åelds of sociology,
communication, marketing, and political science (Rogers
1962; Katz and Lazarsfeld 1955). The notion of inÔ¨Çuence
plays a vital role in how businesses operate and how a society functions‚Äîfor instance, see observations on how fashion
spreads (Gladwell 2002) and how people vote (Berry and
Keller 2003). Studying inÔ¨Çuence patterns can help us better understand why certain trends or innovations are adopted
faster than others and how we could help advertisers and
marketers design more effective campaigns. Studying inÔ¨Çuence patterns, however, has been difÔ¨Åcult. This is because
such a study does not lend itself to readily available quantiÔ¨Åcation, and essential components like human choices and
the ways our societies function cannot be reproduced within
the conÔ¨Ånes of the lab.
Nevertheless, there have been important theoretical studies on the diffusion of inÔ¨Çuence, albeit with radically different results. Traditional communication theory states that

The traditional inÔ¨Çuentials theory has also been criticized
because its information Ô¨Çow process does not take into account the role of ordinary users. In order to compare the
role of inÔ¨Çuentials and ordinary users, researchers have developed a series of simulations, in which information Ô¨Çows

a trend can be initiated by any one, and if the environment is

The above competing ideas have remained as hypotheses
for several reasons. First is the lack of data that could be
used to empirically test them. Although there exist a handful
of empirical studies on word-of-mouth inÔ¨Çuence (Leskovec,
Adamic, and Huberman 2007; Cha, Mislove, and Gummadi
2009), no work has been conducted on the relative order
of inÔ¨Çuence among individuals. A second issue is the variety of ways that inÔ¨Çuence has been deÔ¨Åned (Watts 2007;
Goyal, Bonchi, and Lakshmanan 2010). It has been unclear
what exactly inÔ¨Çuence means. Finally, decades have passed
since the inÔ¨Çuentials theory appeared. Even if the theory
was reasonably accurate when it was proposed, things have
changed and now we have much more variability in the Ô¨Çow
of inÔ¨Çuence. In particular, online communities have become
a signiÔ¨Åcant way we receive new information and inÔ¨Çuence
in such communities needs to be explored. In this paper, we
investigate the notion of inÔ¨Çuence using a large amount of
data collected from a popular social medium, Twitter.

1) Analysis of the three inÔ¨Çuence measures provides a better
understanding of the different roles users play in social
media. Indegree represents popularity of a user; retweets
represent the content value of one‚Äôs tweets; and mentions
represent the name value of a user. Hence, the top users
based on the three measures have little overlap.
2) Our Ô¨Ånding on how inÔ¨Çuence varies across topics could
serve as a useful test for answering how effective advertisement in Twitter would be if one is to employ inÔ¨Çuential
users. Our analysis shows that most inÔ¨Çuential users hold

3) Ordinary users can gain inÔ¨Çuence by focusing on a single topic and posting creative and insightful tweets that
are perceived as valuable by others, as opposed to simply

These Ô¨Åndings provide new insights for viral marketing.
The Ô¨Årst Ô¨Ånding in particular indicates that indegree alone
reveals little about the inÔ¨Çuence of a user. This has been
coined the million follower fallacy by Avnit (Avnit 2009),
who pointed to anecdotal evidence that some users follow

who‚Äôs following you‚Äîand many do not read all the broadcast tweets. We have empirically demonstrated that having a
million followers does not always mean much in the Twitter
world. Instead, we claim that it is more inÔ¨Çuential to have
an active audience who retweets or mentions the user.

DeÔ¨Åning inÔ¨Çuence on Twitter
We start by reviewing studies of diffusion of inÔ¨Çuence and
related work on inÔ¨Çuence propagation on Twitter.

Background
There are a number of conÔ¨Çicting ideas and theories about
how trends and innovations get adopted and spread.
The traditional view assumes that a minority of members in a society possess qualities that make them exceptionally persuasive in spreading ideas to others. These exceptional individuals drive trends on behalf of the majority of ordinary people. They are loosely described as being informed, respected, and well-connected; they are called
the opinion leaders in the two-step Ô¨Çow theory (Katz and
Lazarsfeld 1955), innovators in the diffusion of innovations
theory (Rogers 1962), and hubs, connectors, or mavens in
other work (Gladwell 2002). The theory of inÔ¨Çuentials is
intuitive and compelling. By identifying and convincing a
small number of inÔ¨Çuential individuals, a viral campaign can
reach a wide audience at a small cost. The theory spread well
beyond academia and has been adopted in many marketing
businesses, e.g., RoperASW and Tremor (Gladwell 2002;
Berry and Keller 2003).

Measuring inÔ¨Çuence on Twitter
The Merriam-Webster dictionary deÔ¨Ånes inÔ¨Çuence as ‚Äúthe
power or capacity of causing an effect in indirect or intangible ways.‚Äù Despite the large number of theories of inÔ¨Çuence in sociology, there is no tangible way to measure such
a force nor is there a concrete deÔ¨Ånition of what inÔ¨Çuence
means, for instance, in the spread of news.
In this paper, we analyze the Twitter network as a news
spreading medium and study the types and degrees of inÔ¨Çuence within the network. Focusing on an individual‚Äôs potential to lead others to engage in a certain act, we highlight

Related work on Twitter
Several recent efforts have been made to track inÔ¨Çuence
on Twitter. The Web Ecology Project tracked 12 popular Twitter users for a 10-day period and grouped a user‚Äôs
inÔ¨Çuence into two types: conversation-based and contentbased (Leavitt et al. 2009). This work concluded that news
media are better at spreading content, while celebrities are
better at simply making conversation. Our work extends
their notion of inÔ¨Çuence and uses extensive data to further
examine the spatial and temporal dynamics of inÔ¨Çuence.
More recently, a PageRank-like measure has been proposed to quantify inÔ¨Çuence on Twitter (Weng et al. 2010).
The authors found high link reciprocity (72%) from a nonrandom sample of 6,748 Singapore-based users, and argued
that high reciprocity is indicative of homophily. They then
exploited this fact in computing a user‚Äô inÔ¨Çuence rank. Our
study, however, contradicts the observation about high reciprocity; near-complete data of Twitter shows low reciprocity
(10%). Thus, we predict that social links on Twitter represent an inÔ¨Çuence relationship, rather than homophily. Accordingly, we ask what are the different activities on Twitter
that represent inÔ¨Çuence of a user and to what extent a person‚Äôs inÔ¨Çuence varies across tweet topic and time.

Methodology for comparing user inÔ¨Çuence

each inÔ¨Çuence measure and compared them. Rather than
comparing the values directly, we used the relative order of
users‚Äô ranks as a measure of difference. In order to do this,
we sorted users by each measure, so that the rank of 1 indicates the most inÔ¨Çuential user and increasing rank indicates a less inÔ¨Çuential user. Users with the same inÔ¨Çuence
value receive the average of the rank amongst them (Buck
1980). Once every user is assigned a rank for each inÔ¨Çuence
measure, we are ready to quantify how a user‚Äôs rank varies
across different measures and examine what kinds of users
are ranked high for a given measure.

The rise and fall of inÔ¨Çuentials over time
Many factors‚Äîsocial, political, and economical‚Äîaffect
popularity and inÔ¨Çuence of individuals and organizations. In
online social media, such dynamics is facilitated by easy entry of competition. It only costs 140 characters to generate
a tweet for any user. Likewise, it will be challenging for inÔ¨Çuentials to maintain their status when many emerging local
opinion leaders and evangelists enter the arena.
Here we analyze the dynamics of individual‚Äôs inÔ¨Çuence
over time in two ways. First, we track the popularity of top
inÔ¨Çuentials over a long term period and check how well they
maintain their ranks. Second, we focus on users who increased their inÔ¨Çuence in a speciÔ¨Åc topic over a short time
period, in order to understand what behaviors make ordinary
individuals inÔ¨Çuential.

The rank correlation is generally high (above 0.5) and gets
stronger for the top 1% of users. Mentions show an even
stronger correlation across topics than retweets. This means
that a popular user who is good at spawning mentions from
others can do so over a wide range of topics, more easily
than when she is retweeted over diverse topics. Among topic
pairs, Swine and Jackson showed the highest correlation for
the top 10% of well-connected users for both the retweet and
mention inÔ¨Çuence. This is perhaps due to the more social
nature of these two events, which differs from the Iran topic
where special interest groups like politicians and bloggers
played a major role.
Given that the rank correlation gets stronger for users with
high indegree, we investigated how widely the ranks of the
top listed users change by topic. Figure 3 shows the retweet
ranks of the top users in the Iran topic against the relative
retweet ranks of the same users in the Swine and Jackson
topics. It is clear from the Ô¨Ågure that the top 5 users in
the Iran topic retained their relative ranks in the other two
topics. These users were Mashable, CnnBrk, TweetMeme,
Time, and BreakingNews, who were all in the category of
authoritative news sources and content trackers. The mention inÔ¨Çuence also showed a similar trend (not shown here);
most inÔ¨Çuentials ranked consistently high amongst different
topics. And we have observed this trend not only for the
hyper-inÔ¨Çuentials: moderate inÔ¨Çuentials like opinion leaders and evangelists also had consistent inÔ¨Çuence ranks over
diverse topics, as shown in Table 3.
Our Ô¨Åndings about the highly skewed ability of users to
inÔ¨Çuence others (Figure 2) and the strong correlation in a
user‚Äôs inÔ¨Çuence rank across different topics (Table 3) together lead to two interesting conclusions. First, most inÔ¨Çuential users hold signiÔ¨Åcant inÔ¨Çuence over a variety of topics. This means that local opinion leaders and highly popular
Ô¨Ågures could indeed be used to spread information outside
their area of expertise. In fact, new advertisement campaigns
have recently been launched that insert advertisement links
into a popular person‚Äôs tweet (Fiorillo 2009). Second, the
power-law trend in the difference among inÔ¨Çuence of individuals indicates that it is substantially more effective to target the top inÔ¨Çuentials than to employ a massive number of
non-popular users in order to kick start a viral campaign.

Maintaining engagement of the top inÔ¨Çuentials
Out of all 6 million active users in the Twitter network, we
picked the top 100 users based on each of the three measures: indegree, retweets, and mentions. We used all the
tweets ever posted on Twitter in identifying these inÔ¨Çuentials. Due to the overlap we discussed in Figure 1, there
were 233 distinct users, whom we call all-time inÔ¨Çuentials.
In order to see how the inÔ¨Çuence of these all-time inÔ¨Çuentials varied over time, we tracked their inÔ¨Çuence scores over
an 8 month period from January to August 2009.
To get a time-varying measure of inÔ¨Çuence, we counted
the number of retweets and mentions the all-time inÔ¨Çuentials spawned in every 15 days over the 8 month period.
Because we only know the indegree information based on
the Ô¨Ånal snapshot of the Twitter network, we do not use this
measure. For each user, we computed a single explanatory
variable P: the probability that a random tweet posted on
Twitter during a 15 day period is a retweet (or a mention) of
that user. Normalizing by the total number of tweets posted
on Twitter is essential to cancel out any variable effect on the
data and allows the underlying characteristics of the data sets
to be compared. For instance, because the Twitter network
quadrupled over time in terms of the registered users, the total volume of tweets merely increased over time. Hence, if
we didn‚Äôt normalize the results, the trend wouldn‚Äôt be interesting. Google similarly normalizes the data when analyzing
their search trends (Ginsberg et al. 2009).

retweets and mentions of the 233 all-time inÔ¨Çuential users.

The evolution of the retweet probability in Figure 4(a)
shows that all three groups mildly increased their inÔ¨Çuence
over time. Large error ranges for the top 10 users indicate a
much wider variability in the popularity of this group. The
growth, however, is marginal for all three groups. We conjecture that the marginal increase is due to the limited number of tweets users post a day. Broadcasting too many tweets
puts even popular users at a risk of being classiÔ¨Åed as spammers. Hence, Twitter users should moderate the number of
broadcast tweets in order to avoid crossing their followers‚Äô
information processing limit.
The evolution of the mention probability in Figure 4(b)
shows distinct patterns for the three groups. The top 10
users fell in popularity over time; the next group had consistent inÔ¨Çuence scores over time; and the last group (the least
connected among the three) increased their popularity over
time. Users in the last group, surprisingly, spawned on average more mentions than the top 100 users. While this trend
is counter-intuitive at Ô¨Årst, the differences between mentions
and retweets can explain the trend.
The mainstream news organizations in the Ô¨Årst group are
retweeted the most, but they are not mentioned the most.
This is because their names come up mostly when their content get retweeted; it is hard for media sources to engage
users with their identities alone. The second group, comprised of celebrities, is more often mentioned than retweeted
because of their name value. Their tweets also get retweeted,
when inÔ¨Çuence is transferred across topics (Table 3). Evangelists in the last group successfully increased their inÔ¨Çuence. While many factors could explain this phenomenon,
our manual inspection revealed that these users put signiÔ¨Å-

cant efforts in conversing with others (e.g., replying to their
audience). In a sense, they need self-advertisement the most,
because mass media and celebrities have many other on- and
off-line channels of to promote themselves.
While our Ô¨Åndings provide an interesting view of how different groups of people maintain their popularity, we should
also emphasize that our analysis is in retrospect but not
causal. These Ô¨Åndings are based on the set of users who
ultimately became popular. We also mention that all inÔ¨Çuentials put efforts in posting creative and interesting tweets,
as shown from the high correlation in their retweet ranks.

Conclusions
This paper analyzed the inÔ¨Çuence of Twitter users by employing three measures that capture different perspectives:
indegree, retweets, and mentions. We found that indegree
represents a user‚Äôs popularity, but is not related to other important notions of inÔ¨Çuence such as engaging audience, i.e.,
retweets and mentions. Retweets are driven by the content
value of a tweet, while mentions are driven by the name
value of the user. Such subtle differences lead to dissimilar
groups of the top Twitter users; users who have high indegree do not necessarily spawn many retweets or mentions.
This Ô¨Ånding suggests that indegree alone reveals very little
about the inÔ¨Çuence of a user.
Focusing on retweets and mentions, we studied the dynamics of inÔ¨Çuence across topics and time. Our spatial analysis showed that most inÔ¨Çuential users can hold signiÔ¨Åcant
inÔ¨Çuence over a variety of topics. The top Twitter users
had a disproportionate amount of inÔ¨Çuence, which was indicated by a power-law distribution. Our temporal analysis identiÔ¨Åed how different types of inÔ¨Çuentials interact with
their audience. Mainstream news organizations consistently
spawned a high level of retweets over diverse topics. In contrast, celebrities were better at inducing mentions from their
audience. This is because the name value of the mention inÔ¨Çuentials helped them get responses from others, rather than
any inherent value in the content they posted.
Finally, we found that inÔ¨Çuence is not gained spontaneously or accidentally, but through concerted effort. In order to gain and maintain inÔ¨Çuence, users need to keep great
personal involvement. This could mean that inÔ¨Çuential users
are more predictable than suggested by theory (Watts 2007),
shedding light on how to identify emerging inÔ¨Çuential users.
