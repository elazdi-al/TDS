# Summary: Fifteen Years of Internet Voting in Switzerland

**Title:** Fifteen Years of Internet Voting in Switzerland: History, Governance and Use  
**Authors:** Uwe Serdült, Micha Germann, Fernando Mendez, Alicia Portenier, Christoph Wellig  
**Year:** 2015  
**Source:** 2015 Fourth International Conference on E-Government and E-Systems with implications for Gender and ICT  

## 1. Introduction
This paper reviews the piecemeal introduction of internet voting in Switzerland, a highly federalized political setting. It traces the historical implementation, analyzes the governance networks involved, reports on usage patterns, and examines the socio-demographic profiles of internet voters. 

Switzerland serves as a unique case study due to its decentralized nature and the existing prevalence of postal voting (which took 30 years to generalize). As of 2015, two cantons (Geneva and Neuchâtel) allowed a selection of residents to vote online, and twelve cantons offered internet voting to citizens living abroad.

## 2. History and Implementation Models
The introduction of internet voting in Switzerland has been a slow, piecemeal process starting in the late 1990s. Three distinct models emerged from pilot cantons:

1.  **Zurich Model:** 
    *   Operated by a private company.
    *   No initial registration required; citizens receive login details by mail.
    *   Temporarily halted between 2011-2015 due to technical interface issues (not the voting solution itself).
2.  **Geneva Model:**
    *   Hosted and managed by the canton.
    *   Similar to Zurich in user experience (no prior registration required).
3.  **Neuchâtel Model:**
    *   Integrated into a broader e-government portal.
    *   **Requires registration** at the municipality to use the portal before accessing internet voting.
    *   Allows other transactions like tax filing.

**Expansion to Swiss Abroad:**
A major strategic shift occurred starting in 2006 to focus on Swiss expatriates. This group faces challenges with postal delivery and generally has lower turnout. By 2015, roughly 70% of Swiss expatriates could vote online. 12 out of 26 cantons offered this service, often adopting the Zurich or Geneva systems (forming consortiums) rather than developing their own.

## 3. Governance
The governance of internet voting involves a complex "multi-level governance network" due to Switzerland's federal structure where elections are cantonal/municipal affairs.

*   **Federal Level:** The Federal Chancellery acts as a central coordinator and mentor, overseeing compliance with national norms and financing trials (initially up to 80%).
*   **Cantonal Level:** Responsible for execution and implementation.
*   **Network Analysis:**
    *   **2003-2007 (Phase 1):** The Federal Chancellery was the central node, closely linked with pilot cantons (Geneva, Neuchâtel, Zurich).
    *   **2008-2014 (Phase 2):** Structure remained similar but with increased collaboration density (31%) among cantons and the Federal Chancellery.
    *   Private providers, academia, and NGOs play peripheral roles.

## 4. Usage of Internet Voting
Usage rates vary significantly between domestic residents and expatriates, and between systems.

*   **Domestic Residents:**
    *   **Geneva/Zurich:** Usage rates often hover around **20%**. There is a visible "novelty effect" where usage spikes upon introduction and then stabilizes or declines as voters revert to convenient postal voting.
    *   **Neuchâtel:** Consistently lower usage (**<10%**) due to the barrier of required registration for the e-government portal. Spikes were observed when other features (e.g., electronic tax filing) were added.
*   **Swiss Expatriates:**
    *   Usage is significantly higher, typically **40-60%**, and trending upwards (up to 70% in some cantons).
    *   expatriates appear "loyal" to the online channel, likely due to the unreliability of postal voting from abroad.

## 5. Socio-Demographic Profile of Internet Voters
The paper synthesizes findings from seven studies regarding the profile of internet voters compared to traditional voters.

*   **Age:** Younger and middle-aged voters (30-49) use internet voting disproportionately more than older cohorts (50+).
*   **Gender:** Men are consistently over-represented among internet voters.
*   **Income & Education:** High-income households and individuals with higher education levels are over-represented.

**Key Insight - The Role of ICT Variables:**
Multivariate analyses reveal that socio-demographic variables (age, gender, income, education) **lose their statistical significance** when controlling for ICT variables such as:
*   IT skills
*   Frequency of internet usage
*   Trust in internet transactions

This suggests that internet voting usage is driven by **digital literacy and affinity** rather than demographic characteristics per se. The "digital divide" in voting is thus a reflection of the digital divide in society.

## 6. Outlook
*   **2nd Generation Systems:** Launching in 2015 with "individual verification" mechanisms.
*   **Future:** Internet voting is established and will expand, but full generalization will be slow (similar to the 30-year adoption of postal voting).
*   **Relevance:** The Swiss "laboratory federalism" approach offers insights for other decentralized nations like Australia and Canada.
